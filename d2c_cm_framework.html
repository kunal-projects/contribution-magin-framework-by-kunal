Why a single gross margin number isn't enough for D2C

In a traditional retail business, you hand off inventory to a distributor. The brand's job ends at the factory gate. In D2C, you own the full chain — manufacturing, packaging, warehousing, shipping, returns, acquiring every customer. Each of those layers eats into your revenue in a different way.

The CM1/CM2/CM3 framework layers these costs sequentially so you can see exactly where margin is being consumed — and which levers will actually move the needle.

The three levels answer three distinct questions a founder or analyst needs to separate:

LevelQuestion answeredHealthy range
CM1Is the product itself profitable?50 – 70%
CM2Is the order profitable after fulfilment?30 – 50%
CM3Is the growth engine profitable after marketing?15 – 35%
CM1 is weak
Sourcing or pricing problem. No amount of marketing efficiency will fix it. Renegotiate COGS or reprice.
CM2 is weak
Operational problem. Too heavy on shipping, returns, or 3PL. Volume might help via negotiation.
CM3 is weak
Marketing problem. CAC is too high relative to order value. Focus: ROAS, retention, organic channels.

Salaries, rent, tech subscriptions, brand marketing campaigns. These are fixed — they don't move per order, which is why they sit below CM3.
Key distinctions that trip people up
Variable vs Fixed
Only variable costs go into CM1–CM3. If a cost doesn't change with each order, it belongs in SG&A. Salary of your logistics manager = fixed = below CM3.
Performance vs Brand marketing
Google Ads spend that directly drove this order = CM3. Annual photoshoot for the brand = SG&A. The test: would this cost vanish if you paused new orders?
Gross vs Net revenue
Always use net revenue (after discounts and returns) as your base for all CM% calculations. Using MRP/gross revenue makes margins look falsely healthy.

How to read LTV : CAC

≥ 5× — Excellent. Efficient acquisition, strong repeat. You can scale aggressively.
3× – 5× — Healthy. Sustainable growth. Optimise marketing mix for efficiency.
2× – 3× — Marginal. Cover fixed costs is hard. Focus on improving repeat rate or reducing CAC.
Below 2× — Unsustainable. Every new customer costs more to acquire than they generate in contribution. Growth destroys value.
The fastest way to improve LTV:CAC is growing the repeat rate — returning customers carry near-zero CAC, which dilutes the blended number significantly.
